Graph isomorphism: still hard. The problem is that you're trying to find a bijection between vertex sets that preserves all edge relationships, and there's no efficient algorithm for the general case — you essentially have to try candidates. Under time pressure in a quiz or exam, I can see myself freezing. I know what I'm looking for but the search feels unstructured.

TumTum noted that graph isomorphism is a known hard problem (GI-complete, possibly not in P) and that for exam purposes the graphs will be small enough to check by hand — but that having a systematic approach matters. It suggested: check degree sequences first (necessary condition), then look for structural landmarks (high-degree vertices, triangles, bridges). I practiced this on three example pairs. Got two right, one wrong because I missed a triangle.

Euler paths and circuits were more satisfying. The handshake theorem is clean — the sum of all degrees equals twice the number of edges, so the number of odd-degree vertices is always even. An Euler circuit requires all vertices to have even degree. An Euler path (not circuit) requires exactly two odd-degree vertices. I can prove both from the handshake theorem.

Trees feel intuitive. Connected graph with no cycles. I like them.
